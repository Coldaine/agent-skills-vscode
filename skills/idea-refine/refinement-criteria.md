# Refinement & Evaluation Criteria

Use this rubric to assess how well an idea has been refined and whether it's ready to act on. It's not a scoring system — it's a structured way to identify what's still missing.

---

## Dimension 1: Problem Clarity

**What we're assessing:** Is the problem being solved well-understood?

### Level 1 — Weak
- Problem is described in terms of the solution ("we need X" rather than "people struggle with Y")
- Problem exists but hasn't been validated with anyone who actually has it
- Scale and frequency of the problem are unknown

### Level 2 — Developing
- Problem is described independently of the solution
- Some evidence it's real (user feedback, support tickets, observation)
- Rough sense of scale — affects this type of user in this kind of situation

### Level 3 — Strong
- Problem is stated precisely: who has it, when, what's the consequence of not solving it
- Evidence comes from multiple sources (data + qualitative)
- Scale is quantified or estimated with confidence
- The right people agree this problem matters and is worth solving

---

## Dimension 2: Solution Definition

**What we're assessing:** Is the proposed solution specific enough to evaluate?

### Level 1 — Weak
- Solution is described at a concept level only ("AI will handle it", "we'll automate it")
- No one can describe what a user actually does with it
- Multiple incompatible interpretations exist in the group

### Level 2 — Developing
- User flow exists at a high level
- Key decisions have been made (what it does, who uses it)
- Gaps remain in non-obvious cases (errors, edge cases, secondary users)

### Level 3 — Strong
- Primary user flow is fully articulated
- Edge cases considered and resolved (or explicitly deferred)
- Clear what the solution is NOT doing
- Someone new could build this without needing to make major decisions

---

## Dimension 3: Feasibility

**What we're assessing:** Can this be built with available resources?

### Level 1 — Weak
- Technical feasibility not considered
- No estimate of effort, cost, or time
- Requires capabilities the team doesn't have and hasn't acquired

### Level 2 — Developing
- High-level technical approach identified
- Rough effort estimate exists (weeks vs. months vs. quarters)
- Known unknowns identified, not yet resolved

### Level 3 — Strong
- Technical approach is specific and realistic
- Effort estimate is reliable (from someone who will build it)
- Major technical risks identified with mitigation plans
- Dependencies on other teams or systems are acknowledged and managed

---

## Dimension 4: Desirability

**What we're assessing:** Do real users actually want this?

### Level 1 — Weak
- Desirability is assumed, not validated
- "If we build it, they will come" reasoning
- The people who want this are internal stakeholders, not users

### Level 2 — Developing
- Some user feedback exists (even if informal)
- Confidence that the target user has the problem
- Uncertainty about whether this solution is the one users would choose

### Level 3 — Strong
- Target users have been asked about the problem AND the solution
- Evidence of pull: users have asked for something like this, or would use it now if available
- Willingness to pay / change behavior has been assessed (not just expressed interest)

---

## Dimension 5: Viability

**What we're assessing:** Does this make sense from a business / organizational perspective?

### Level 1 — Weak
- Business case not considered
- No view on revenue, cost savings, or strategic value
- Organizational readiness not assessed

### Level 2 — Developing
- Strategic rationale exists (this fits our goals because...)
- Rough estimate of impact (even directional)
- Known organizational dependencies

### Level 3 — Strong
- Clear value proposition with quantified or estimable impact
- Business model alignment is explicit (how this creates or preserves value)
- Organizational readiness assessed (who needs to support this, and do they?)
- Trade-offs with other priorities acknowledged

---

## Dimension 6: Risk Profile

**What we're assessing:** Are the risks understood and acceptable?

### Level 1 — Weak
- Risks haven't been considered
- Major assumptions are unexamined
- No plan for what to do if key assumptions are wrong

### Level 2 — Developing
- Main risks have been identified
- Key assumptions listed
- Some thought given to mitigation or monitoring

### Level 3 — Strong
- Load-bearing assumptions explicitly identified and ranked
- High-uncertainty assumptions have a testing plan
- Worst-case scenario is understood and acceptable (or there's a stop-loss plan)
- The team has genuinely considered whether this could fail and how

---

## Readiness Assessment

Use this to decide what to do next:

| Profile | Recommendation |
|---------|----------------|
| All dimensions Level 3 | Ready to proceed to planning/execution |
| Most Level 2, no Level 1 | Proceed with defined open questions; resolve during execution |
| Any dimension Level 1 | Stop and address before proceeding |
| Problem at Level 1 | Don't evaluate solution — reframe the problem first |
| Feasibility at Level 1 | Don't commit resources — get a technical assessment first |

---

## Using This Rubric in Practice

This rubric works best as a conversation tool, not a scoring exercise. Walk through each dimension and ask: where are we on this? What would it take to get to Level 3?

Some dimensions matter more depending on the stakes:
- **Low-stakes experiments**: Feasibility and desirability are most critical — build cheap, learn fast
- **High-investment initiatives**: All dimensions should reach Level 3 before major resource commitment
- **Strategic bets**: Viability and risk profile deserve extra scrutiny even with uncertainty elsewhere

A idea that's Level 2 across all dimensions is often ready to proceed with clear checkpoints. An idea that's Level 1 in any dimension needs more work before proceeding.
